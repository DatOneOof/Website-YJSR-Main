HOW DOES BLOOD CLOT

HOW DO BLOOD COAGULATION TESTS WORK?

Posted by Zehra Polat on August 28, 2023

Introduction

There are 2 types of blood clotting in medicine. Pathological and non-pathological. Also known as Intrinsic and Extrinsic systems. And PT/INR is the coagulation test that is used most commonly. It evaluates only extrinsic related coagulation. INR stands for International Normalized Ratio. It is a calculation based on PT (Prothrombin Time) values indicating a blood sample’s ability to clot and how long it takes. PT/INR tests are usually evaluated on patients who are being treated with a blood-thinning medication also known as anticoagulant medication warfarin. Also, there is bleeding time, platelet count, complete blood count, factor 5 assay, thrombin time, and fibrinogen count. We will talk about them more in the next segments.

EXTRINSIC AND INTRINSIC SYSTEM RELATED COAGULATION

Non-Pathological blood clot ability is all about your blood sample and its values. How we understand your blood sample’s ability to clot is that we look at 13 values that are also known as the 13 Coagulation Factors. But there are only twelve non-pathological coagulation factors. The sixth one is unassigned because it was realized that it was part of another factor. And we will explain it in another segment.

The factors of Pathological coagulation depend on are:



1. Damage to the Endothelium

The endothelium is a barrier that is made out of endothelial cells. It creates an interface between blood and the blood vessels. It has a lot of functions. It supports the blood vessels and stops blood from interacting with blood vessels. It can get damaged and decrease its diameter from lipids around it, or physical damage to the vessel.

2. Stasis.

Stasis happens when blood circulation in the circulatory system is slower than usual. This can be caused by rest, old age, or other reasons.

3. The 13 Coagulation Factors

This Reason depends entirely on the blood sample’s values, unlike other reasons that usually depend on the vascular system. So it is entirely about non-pathological aka intrinsic system-related coagulation. These factors are:

  • Factor 1-Fibrinogen
  • Factor 2- Prothrombin
  • Factor 4- Ionized Calcium
  • Factor 5- Labile Factor or Proaccelerin
  • Factor 6- No Factor
  • Factor 7- Stable Factor or Proconvertin
  • Factor 8- Antihemophilic Factor
  • Factor 9- Plasma Thromboplastin Component, Christmas Factor
  • Factor 10- Stuart-Prower Factor
  • Factor 11- Plasma Thromboplastin Antecedent
  • Factor 12- Hageman Factor
  • Factor 13- Fibrin-Stabilizing Factor

WHAT DO COAGULATION TESTS DETECT?

So there are a total of 3 reasons why coagulation tests are taken:

1. Liver Disease:

Our liver plays a huge role in coagulation. Many of the 13 coagulation factors and coagulation tests can be associated with liver disease. For example, the first of the 13 coagulation factors, fibrinogen is a protein produced by the liver. Or a low level of Factor 5 may indicate liver disease. Factor 2, Prothrombin is a protein that is also produced by the liver.

2. Thrombophilia

Thrombophilia is excessive clotting. It may cause aneurysms and block blood vessels. It is usually because of a genetic mutation that damages the factors for clotting.

3. Hemophilia

Hemophilia is clotting less than enough. Even though it is a rare genetic disorder and it doesn’t make up a big problem in daily life it can be very dangerous even in small cuts.

COAGULATION TESTS

There is a lot of coagulation in medicine but we don’t usually use them all together. We use PT/INR as the “main” one. Those are the coagulation tests we currently use:

  • Factor 5 Assay

    Factor 5 assay or as commonly known Factor V ( Labile Factor or Proaccelerin) assay is a cofactor, unlike other factors that are mostly proteins. It is a cofactor to activate the protein prothrombin to turn it into thrombin. The unusual lowness of Factor V can cause not enough clotting (hemophilia) and as a result of that bleeding.


  • Platelet Count

    Platelet Count shows the number of platelet cells in your blood. Normal values should be between 150,000 to 450,000 for every microliter of your blood sample. Platelet cells are cells that helps blood to clot. The unusual lowness of Platelet count may cause hemophilia and an excessive amount of platelet cells can cause thrombophilia.


  • Complete Blood Count- CBC

    As you can understand from its name Complete Blood Count tests evaluate the complete value of the blood. CBC contains:

    1. Red blood cells
    2. White blood cells
    3. Hemoglobin
    4. Hematocrit
    5. Platelets


  • Thrombin Time

    As we mentioned before thrombin is an enzyme that allows blood to clot. Thrombin time is a test that evaluates how long the blood sample takes to clot in a blood sample that has been given anti-coagulant medicines and after added thrombin. Thrombin time is usually used after an abnormal level of thromboplastin time (aPTT) or prothrombin time (PT). A normal thrombin time is around 12 – 19 seconds.

  • Bleeding Time

    Bleeding time is a test that is easy as it sounds. Your doctor or nurse makes a couple of little scratches in your arm and measures how long it takes to clot. And it is usually around 1 – 9 minutes.

  • aPTT

    APTT is a test where we add calcium and phospholipids and a contact agent to the blood sample that activates Factor 12 also known as Hageman Factor.

  • Prothrombin Time (PT/INR)

    PT and INR are normally two separate tests. We use PT as an indicator of your blood sample’s ability to clot by adding phospholipid, tissue factor (factor 3), and calcium to the blood sample. And see how long it takes to clot. INR is a calculation just based on PT results. PT/INR is mostly used for intrinsic systems. PT/INR indicates Factor 2, 7, and Factor 10, Prothrombin and fibrinogen values, and is also used for Vitamin K antagonists. PT/INR is usually used for patients who are treated with the blood-thinning medicine warfarin.

CONCLUSION

Intrinsic Coagulation depends on 12 values, also known as the 13 coagulation factors. But Extrinsic Coagulation depends on 3 things; The 13 coagulation factors, stasis, and endothelium. There are a lot of coagulation tests on the market but the most commonly used one is PT/INR tests which are usually used on patients that are with blood-thinning medication warfarin.
