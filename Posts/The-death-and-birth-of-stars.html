Stars

The death and birth of stars

Posted by Nour Al Imane TOUFIQ on August 5, 2023

Introduction

Have you ever looked at the night sky and just found yourself mesmerized by its vastness and the depth of its shining sky lanterns? I know I have, and I always wondered how on earth such extraordinary actually exists. How is a star born? And for that matter, how does it die? In this article, we are going to tackle down the formation of stars, from the gathering of interstellar clouds, to the hydrostatic equilibrium and formation of protostars and finally the nuclear fusion that actually forms the star the second part of this article explains the life sequence of stars and the aftermath of their death according to their masses. And we will sum u by a conclusion at the end

Gas clouds / Interstellar clouds

There are gas atoms of helium and hydrogen scattered through space, and due to their mass (Newton's gravitational law) and Einstein's relativity, the atoms attract each other and condense into a big cloud of gas and dust. Thus, the more it gathers dust and particles, the more it adds to its mass and therefore gathers more gravitational forces, which pull them evenly towards the centre, shaping a spherical object. As the particles bump into each other, they create more friction and heat. Then the cloud turns into a protostar with not enough gravitational force to create a fusion reaction yet, but as it stabilises in the hydro-static equilibrium state, it gathers more dust and elements. As it gains about 80% of the solar mass and reaches a temperature of about 107 Kelvins, it starts fusing the hydrogen in the core by a process called the proton-proton fusion chain, and that’s how a star is born.

Nuclear fusion

There are billions of trillions of stars in our universe that differ in color and size and we might wonder why is that? To tackle this we need to understand how the stars shine.

Stars are big, like really big, as are their gravitational forces that are so huge they attract the hydrogen and helium atoms to the core and smash them together, creating bigger elements by nuclear fusion. that emits enormous amounts of energy and radiation that sends light to planets like our planet Earth. But the sizes of the stars differ; the bigger the star, the bigger the nuclear forces it creates. And their colour changes according to that. For instance, we can notice the electromagnetic spectrum’s visible light, how the colour of the star changes according to their size, thus the energy they emit is higher (frequency = higher energy).

Energy consumption in low mass stars

The low mass stars are those that contain a mass lower than 8 solar masses, the fusion in the core of the star consumes the fuel (the hydrogen) with time. Yet it contains the same size, temperature and luminosity until most of it is gone. Then, the core of the star starts shrinking and it fuses the rest of the hydrogen faster and that energy emitted grows and the star gains its size and becomes a Red Giant.

The star stays in the red giant state for about a billion years and during that time the core fuses the helium into carbon and oxygen through the triple alpha process due to the high temperature, with time the star then increases and decreases its size periodically pulsating until it fuses the rest of the elements it becomes bluer and hotter. The core contains mostly carbon and oxygen with a thin layer of helium and hydrogen, so the core shrinks more but the outer layer expands through the amount of energy released, and the red giant becomes a planetary nebula with a dense hot core in a size close to earth’s called a white dwarf. If the dwarf gains more mass from nearby objects then the electrons won't be able to push against the gravity anymore and it turns to a supernova.

Energy consumption in High mass stars

The high mass stars are those who have a mass equal or superior to 8 solar masses (the mass of our sun 1.9891x 10^30 kg) and due to the high mass and temperature the hydrogen fusion happens quicker and like the low mass stars it fuses the smaller nuclei to bigger ones and that creates so much energy to the outer layers that it become a red supergiant. Or if the mass of the star is high enough (40 solar masses) they become a blue giant then the star will fuse the nuclei to heavier elements up to iron at this point the core is stable and there aren't much fusion left for the star to fight the gravitational forces and it collapses towards its center that even the electro-repulsive forces of the electrons can’t resist and by that creating such an enormous amount of energy and heat expressed and a bang called the supernova, during this explosion most of the elements of the universe are emitted.

The afterwards of the supernova

There are two cases after the supernova and they depend on the mass of the remnant of the core. if it is between 1.4 and 3 solar masses then it becomes a Neutron Star; that happens because the atomic forces can no longer fight the gravitational pull that even the electrons fuse into the protons in the electron capture and the core turns to this big ball of neutrons and neutrinos and soup of neutron plasma.

Pulsars are neutron stars that orbit around a fixed axis so they emit pulses of electromagnetic waves.

Magnetars are big neutron stars with enormous magnetic fields that range around 10 billion Tesla.

The black-hole

If the remnants of the supernova are higher than 3 solar masses then nothing left can resist the gravitational pull and the core crash to a single point with no volume and a density that adds up to infinity we call it the infinite singularity, creating one of the most wonderful phenomena in the universe: a black hole . According to einstein’s theory of relativity any system with mass curvatures space-time, and the higher the mass is the more gravitational pull that system has to its surroundings,so for a black hole with an infinite density and mass nothing can escape the gravitational field it creates, not even light once arrived at the event horizon the glowing part spinning around the black hole is called the accretion disk which is whatever gas, dust or particles that remain of any celestial object the black hole devours and shreds only adding more mass to its singularity Until now scientists haven't figured out what's beyond the event horizon since nothing can come back from it. In 2019 NASA released the first picture of a blackhole detected accidentally by The Event Horizon Telescope (EHT).

Conclusion

The blackholes open up a whole new field of studies in physics; scientists haven't figured out what’s beyond the event horizon or further information about the infinite singularity yet. The death of stars creates the chance for the birth of new stars, the explosion of the supernova and the planetary nebula creates more dust and gas that contributes to the birth of new ones.
