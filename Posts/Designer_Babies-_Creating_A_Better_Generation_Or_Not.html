Designer Babies: Creating a Better Generation, Or Not?

Posted by Emel Çırakoğlu on June 16, 2023

The medical field is experiencing huge development. With the help of technology, everything seems possible now. One of the fields that are affected by these new impacts is genetics. Genetics has become even more popular since CRISPR’s big success in cutting and proofreading DNA. Scientists now have the power to control our DNA. However, this brings up some questions: Is it ethical to use CRISPR to alter an embryo's DNA?

Even if we don’t know the real answer to this question, we know it is possible. One such way to do so is through IVF.

IVF (in vitro fertilization) is one of the applications of genetics. It is used to help people with fertility problems. First, certain drugs are given to the mother to create many eggs. These eggs are then removed from the mother’s ovaries. The same goes for the father. Some sperm are taken from the father (even though it is still controversial, sperm donors can be used). Fertilization occurs in laboratory settings. After fertilization, the embryo is returned to the mother’s womb. Sometimes development can occur just as expected, but sometimes it doesn’t. The success rate is over 50% in most cases.

This brings up the debates. Changing a baby’s character or intelligence is regarded as "too advanced," but some people think it can be a good thing to have perfect babies. This is a big debate among scientists. Advocates of using genetic applications to alter DNA believe creating a better generation can have a positive effect on the population. For example, research in the USA showed that this can reduce poverty to %25, humanitarian aid to %18, and the crime rate to %25. There won’t be any diseases, aging, poverty, humanitarian problems, or many more. On the contrary, this can also create many cons

One of these cons is human evolution. If people start having babies with IVF just to have "perfect babies," there won’t be any natural fertilization. This will reduce diversity among people since fertilization won’t occur the way it needs to. Only the perfect ones would be selected, while others would be discarded. How will the evolution of our species be affected?

Another thing we need to consider is that we can’t certainly know that designer babies won’t be any different from normal babies. Discarding some genes and replacing them with new ones can have a reverse effect; many genes in our DNA are associated with each other. We can’t know that discarding a few genes won’t create a bigger problem.

All these things mean only one thing: we must use our ability to interfere with DNA wisely. Using CRISPR to prevent diseases would be a scientific breakthrough if only used for this purpose. This technology shouldn’t be used with bad intentions. To prevent this from happening, scientists must make international agreements to control this rapidly increasing new approach.

Taking control of our future generation may seem far away now, but in the next 10 or 20 years, this won’t be a sci-fi scenario anymore.
