Lessons Learned From Covid-19

A Global Crisis, A Global Response

Posted by Kamal Korrapati on August 15, 2023 Edited by Shlok Panchal

Introduction

The COVID-19 pandemic has exposed the vulnerabilities of global and national health systems, especially in developing countries. However, it also presents an opportunity to learn from the crisis and build more resilient health systems through innovation. The pandemic response in developing countries revealed the importance of rapid action to manage noncommunicable disease (NCD) care challenges. Designated COVID-19 facilities allowed NCD patients without COVID-19 symptoms to receive necessary medical services, and extended prescription policies ensured uninterrupted medication access. This rapid pandemic response helped buy valuable time for developing countries to tackle NCD management challenges.

Figure 1: Symptons of Covid-19

A three-pronged approach is suggested to create more resilient health systems: focusing on people, prevention, and technology. Integrated health systems can improve the accessibility, quality, and efficiency of healthcare. Successful approaches include people-centred care models, early screening and diagnosis through community-level primary care, and integration with digital technology. Incorporating a patient-centred approach is crucial in integrated health systems. Countries like China have implemented Chest Pain Centers, Metabolic Disease Management Centers, and Cough and Wheezing Management Centers, significantly improving disease management and treatment efficiency. By reshaping the diagnosis and treatment process in a people-centred way, healthcare systems can enhance patient outcomes and experiences.

Early detection and diagnosis of diseases like cancer can be achieved through evidence-based prevention strategies and widespread screening programs. Developing countries like Mexico, Brazil, Colombia, and Egypt have improved public awareness and access to screening services. Focusing on early detection empowers healthcare systems to address health challenges proactively. Innovative technology, such as telemedicine and AI-assisted decision-making tools, has proven effective during the pandemic. Telemedicine platforms like Halodoc in Indonesia have facilitated 24/7 access to doctor consultations and quick medication delivery. AI technology has the potential to address doctor shortages and improve diagnosis accuracy, leading to better patient outcomes.

To address the urgent challenge of NCDs, collaboration among governments, private-sector industries, and international organizations is essential. Learning from successful strategies and sharing knowledge can help countries benefit from cost-effective and globally applicable NCD interventions regardless of income level. The COVID-19 pandemic serves as a wake-up call, urging the world to unite and align efforts toward creating more resilient health systems. In the face of a pandemic, seeking diverse voices becomes crucial in making informed decisions and addressing health disparities. The lesson for future pandemics lies in tapping into expertise from across the government and society. This includes theologians, communications experts, medical anthropologists, and more. Angela Merkel's approach in intentionally surrounding herself with diverse minds and thoughts is commendable, as it enables leaders to weigh all aspects of the situation and make more effective decisions in a crisis that affects all facets of society.

Covid-19 vaccines

This deliberate pursuit of diversity is also vital in combating health disparities, as exemplified by Massachusetts' health equity advisory group. By involving community members, healthcare workers, and other experts, the state was able to deploy limited resources and develop a successful vaccination strategy effectively. Massachusetts noticed that mass vaccination sites were not reaching all residents equally, prompting them to adopt a hyperlocal approach in vulnerable regions. Hiring community members as vaccine ambassadors, who engaged with residents door-to-door, helped build trust and increase vaccination rates. Engagement with communities should not be transactional, merely focused on administering vaccines and leaving. Instead, public health departments should establish interdisciplinary advisory committees representing the community's diversity. By prioritizing the community's lived experiences and continuously investing in their success, trust and equity can be fostered, whether in addressing lung cancer or preparing for future pandemics. This approach enables leaders to make more inclusive and effective decisions that benefit all members of society.

Conclusion

COVID-19 has taught us that rapid response, patient-centred care, early detection, and embracing innovative technology are crucial elements of resilient health systems. By integrating these lessons into healthcare practices, developing countries can pave the way for a healthier and more prepared future. The pandemic has given us a transformative path to build more robust and adaptable health systems capable of tackling future health challenges.
