Scientists use plasma to produce oxygen on Mars

Are we one step closer to living on the red planet?

Posted by Maria Eduarda de Mattos Brandão on February 28, 2023

A group of scientists conducted research on the production of oxygen on Mars using plasma. At first, the technique could be used to facilitate the movement of humans on the red planet, process fuel, create building materials and produce fertilizers for cultivation on Mars.

As the Martian atmosphere has a high concentration of carbon dioxide, several studies have already been undertaken in order to extract the oxygen gas from this substance. This process consists of two stages: first, the CO2 molecule is broken, separating the oxygen atoms from the carbon; then oxygen needs to be isolated from the mixture obtained.

However, this process presents some difficulties. CO2 molecules are quite stable and therefore require a high amount of energy to be broken down. Furthermore, it is laborious to separate the oxygen gas from the gaseous mixture, which contains carbon dioxide and carbon monoxide molecules.

To try to overcome these challenges, the group of researchers used plasma as a mediator in the process. This material is formed when a hot gas is electrically charged, and it behaves differently from solids, liquids or gasses. The free electrons present in the plasma can be easily accelerated until they are highly energized and, when colliding with carbon dioxide molecules, can decompose them or make them vibrate, facilitating the breakdown of the molecule. Furthermore, the heat generated in the plasma aids in the separation of oxygen from other gasses.

In addition to the expected benefits for the colonization of Mars, the technique can collaborate with mitigating the effects of climate change on planet Earth. The carbon dioxide molecules present in the Earth's atmosphere, currently in much greater amounts than ideal, can be decomposed for the production of biofuels and recycling of chemical products.

Although it is great progress, the human landing on Mars still has many challenges to overcome, such as radiation exposure, low gravity and the lack of water.
