THE MATHEMATICS OF CHESS

Posted by Gülin Gürbüz on August 25, 2023 Edited by Mukund Gupta

Introduction

Welcome to the captivating world of chess, where kings and queens, knights and rooks, bishops, and pawns engage in battle on a checkered battlefield. Beyond the intricate pieces and the hardwood board lies a fascinating mathematical intricacy that has intrigued minds for millennia. Let's delve into the depths of chess's mathematical underpinnings.

Chess Rating System

Chess is a game of strategy and skill. But how do we measure a player's ability in this complex game? That's where chess ratings come into play. In this article, you will learn about the Elo rating system, how it works, and what it means for players. Let's begin. The Elo rating system was developed by Arpad Elo, an American physics professor and chess master. It assigns a numerical value to each player, representing their skill level and probability of winning a game. The International Chess Federation sets the minimum rating at 100, while the highest rating ever achieved is 2882 by Magnus Carlsen.. The rating difference between two players may indicate the outcome of a match. When two players with equal ratings compete, they should win the same number of games. When there is a rating difference, however, the anticipated score for the stronger player rises. A player with a rating 100 points greater than their opponent, for example, is predicted to score approximately 64%. The K-factor plays an important role in rating adjustments. It determines the impact of each game on a player's rating. Higher K-factors result in more significant rating changes, while lower K-factors lead to more stable ratings. Players who exceed expectations are rewarded, while those who fall short are punished. Let's consider an example: a player with a rating of 1000 and a K-factor of 20. If their expected chance of winning a game is 20% ( = 0.2) and they actually win the game ( = 1), their new rating would be calculated as follows: 1000 + 20(1 - 0.2) = 1016. In this case, the player gains 16 points for surpassing expectations. Conversely, losing a game that was expected to be won can result in a significant loss of points. The magnitude of rating changes is influenced by the difference in ratings between players. The larger the rating difference, the fewer points are gained or lost by the higher-rated player, as the expected outcome is more predictable.

The Knight’s Tour Problem

The Knight's Tour is a popular chess puzzle. The challenge is to devise a move sequence that allows a knight to visit every square on a chessboard exactly once. The knight can move in an L-shaped pattern, with two squares in one direction (vertically or horizontally) and one square in the opposite way. The problem can be represented as a graph, with each square on the chessboard representing a vertex, and knight's moves representing edges between the vertices. The task is to find a Hamiltonian cycle -a cycle that visits every vertex exactly once- in this graph. While the task may appear straightforward on an 8x8 chessboard, the complexity climbs exponentially as the board size increases; that is for sure an exciting brainteaser.

Now that we know more about it, let’s move on with Schwenk’s Theorem, which is a significant result in graph theory related to the Knight's Tour problem. David Schwenk proved that for any m × n board with m ≤ n, a closed knight's tour is always possible unless one or more of these three conditions are met:

  1. m and n are both odd
  2. m = 1, 2, or 4
  3. m = 3 and n = 4, 6, or 8.

The theorem establishes both a necessary and sufficient condition for the existence of a closed Knight's Tour on a certain sort of chessboard. Furthermore, it helps focus efforts on finding solutions for those sizes. The theorem has practical applications in chess studies and is used as a starting point for researching the Knight's Tour problem on different board dimensions.
