Rewilding the earth

A necessary path to a better tomorrow

Posted by Xyrene Amansec on August 5, 2023

Introduction

The world is incessantly facing the consequences of human-driven environmental emergencies such as climate change. In the many proposed solutions of mitigating the change in climate, rewilding has come up as one of the most propitious. But, there is always more to it than what meets the eye. The restoration of megafaunas through trophic rewilding is a controversial yet opportune approach to address ecological degradation.

The greenhouse effect is the main driver of climate change. This effect is natural, but it is amplified by greenhouse gas emissions which, as a result, traps more heat inside the earth’s atmosphere, causing global repercussions. According to the Center for Climate and Energy Solutions or C2ES, carbon dioxide emissions (primarily from the combustion of fossil fuels) have risen dramatically since the start of the industrial revolution; And carbon dioxide accounts for about 76 percent of total greenhouse gas emissions.

Trophic rewilding, and ecological restoration efforts in general, came about to aid in increasing carbon sequestration, promoting biodiversity, and improving the carbon stocks of forests and ecosystems around the world. There is mounting evidence that the widespread loss of wildlife, often known as defaunation, affects not only the animals themselves but also the vital functions they play in ecosystems. So rewilding is a novel ecological restoration method that has been developed to replace these lost functions. It focuses on reintroducing missing major animal species or, in the event that they become extinct, their proxies in an effort to restore natural processes in ecosystems as a whole.

Is it an effective and sustainable climate solution?

By safeguarding and expanding carbon capture and storage in plants, soils, and sediments in ecosystems, natural climate solutions are being pushed to stop climate change. In addition to conserving animal species diversity, these solutions are seen as having a secondary benefit of preserving habitats and landscapes. This reasoning, however, undervalues the part that animals play in regulating the carbon cycle. In a recent study, scientific proof has been presented on how preserving wild animals and their ecological functions can improve natural carbon capture and storage.(Schmitz et al., 2023) We must acknowledge the restoration and preservation of wild animals and their ecological functions as an essential part of natural climate solutions that can improve our efforts to prevent global warming beyond 1.5 °C. Since trophic rewilding relies heavily on reinstating existing ecological dynamics and natural processes that have been maintained for years and years, it is most definitely sustainable and adequately long-term.

Potential risks

However, there are still risks involved. What makes trophic rewilding controversial is the possibility of exotic species proliferation as a consequence of rewilding. Rewilding varies from simple reintroduction in that it focuses primarily on restoring species that have a high capacity to exert impact across several trophic levels, with the expectation that these species will have drastically significant and advantageous effects on communities and other organisms and habitats. The concern is that the consequences of introducing a predator or dominating herbivore on a population or ecosystem are difficult to foresee with precision. It is suggested by Nogués-Bravo et al. (2016) that those that favor of trophic rewilding should properly take into account its potential for long-lasting effects and promote basic research that presents potential future conditions of ecosystems following rewilding.

It is also important to recognize that rewilding promotes novel approaches to land management that are centered on re-establishing the independent, diversity-maintaining mechanisms already found in nature. If successful, trophic rewilding could provide higher resistance and resilience to the effects from climate change by restoring more robust ecosystems. (Bakker & Svenning, 2018)
