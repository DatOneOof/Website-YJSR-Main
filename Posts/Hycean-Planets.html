Hycean Planets

The Cosmic Oceans, Exploring Alien life in worlds of water

Posted by Aditya Singh on September 4, 2023 Edited by Aarna Sahu

Introduction


Since the beginning of the human race, we have yearned to seek out others like us; to study each other, communicate, and work together to flourish as a species. However, it is now the 21st century. As modern technology thrives, and our understanding of the universe deepens, we search for life beyond this planet; the idea of ‘aliens’ has fascinated scientists for centuries. And now, we are closer than ever to uncover this cosmic conjecture.

Propelling us further into this study of extraterrestrial life is the developing idea of Hycean planets: Ginormous celestial bodies, made of oceans far preceding those of Earth. Made mostly of water, these worlds seem fit to host life. With various, promising candidates already being studied, and future missions being planned, Hycean planets could very well just be the next, crucial step in our quest to understand the unknown.

The Hypothesis

The study of exoplanets (any planetary body outside the solar system) has been going on for decades; thousands have been found, and thousands more wait to be noticed. However, the idea of the Hycean planet emerged relatively recently. The term was first used in the August edition of the Astrophysical Journal, 2021. In the piece, Cambridge researchers proposed a new category of exoplanets, made almost entirely of hydrogen and water, hence coining the term ‘Hycean’ (hydrogen and ocean). The thought of it intrigued scientists and the public alike. Studies were swiftly conducted to understand, refine, and remodel what counts as an ideal Hycean planet. By analyzing the necessary atmospheric conditions, paired with other contributing factors, a surprising number of exoplanets match the description, the most prominent example being K2-18b (see below). But what exactly qualifies a planet as ‘Hycean’?


Composition


The generic model of a Hycean planet can be directly compared to Earth’s: A core made of a similar ratio of iron and silicate, as well as an atmosphere rich in hydrogen, helium and water vapour. Albeit these resemblances, much more distinguishes the worlds than familiarizes. More than 90% of the planet is ocean; water levels plunge tens of thousands of kilometers deep, eventually condensing into an icy mantle (mainly composed of the monoclinic ‘Ice V’ phase of water). This means that Hycean planets can range up to ten times the mass of our globe. Furthermore, the H2 in the atmosphere, paired with the planet’s tidal lock to its star results in the presence of enormous heat and pressure. In fact, the atmospheric pressure is so high, water can remain liquid and temperatures surpassing 500K. If these conditions could virtually destroy any living creature on Earth, how exactly can life find a way in the expanse of these gigantic oceans?

Alien Life?

There are several factors that go into play when considering how fit Hycean planets are for sustaining life, and they go as follows.

  • The Presence of Organic Chemicals: Hycean planets contain a soup of various bioessential substances that can serve as building blocks for life, the most essential of these being oxygen and methane. Other organic molecules present like dimethysulfide, methanethiol, and carbonylsulfide, while not exclusive to life, could be part of a complex interplay of chemical reactions involving living organisms.

  • The Wide Habitable Zone: The ‘Goldilocks zone’ refers to the region around a star where conditions are just right for liquid water to form on the planet’s surface. Hycean planets are unique for their unusually large Goldilocks zones, which result in a variety of different environmental conditions that can potentially sustain life.

  • Dark vs Cold Hycean Planets: Dark Hycean planets are those which are tidally locked to their star, with the asymmetric environment only allowing life-like conditions to occur on the dark side. With the absence of light, the biosignatures we may receive from these worlds would be very different from our own. On the flip side, there are cold Hycean planets, where internal heat is enough to battle harsh conditions like their large distance from their star.

  • Potential for Complexity: Given the extreme conditions, it is plausible that any life form present will remain microbial in nature. However, given the distinct chemical composition of such planets, alternative evolutionary paths could eventually give rise to multicellular life forms that are fundamentally extraordinary.

The Future?

Although no such worlds have been confirmed to exist, our understanding of them comes from a huge number of potential candidates. If the maths checks out, the current number of Hycean planets outnumber Gas giants and Terrestrial planets combined. There is a possibilty that we are studying a Hycean planet at this very moment; the mini-Neptune K218b (which is a mere 124 light years from earth) has water vapour, and is a prime subject in the search for habitable planets. Various biosignature searches are yet to be conducted, including the use of the famous James Webb telescope to detect and analyze Hycean planets. For now, with whatever information we have, it is easy to say that Hycean worlds are very different from our own Earth. But they remarkably prove that despite being so exclusive, they can still be home to life.
