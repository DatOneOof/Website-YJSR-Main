The Dopamine Hypothesis of Schizophrenia

Posted by Charvi Kashyap on January 13, 2023

Abstract

The most popular theory of schizophrenia to date is around the dopamine hypothesis (Howes and Kapur, 2009). Schizophrenia's "overstimulation of dopamine receptors could be part of the etiology" according to Jacques Van Rossum's 1966 theory.

By discovering that the effectiveness of antipsychotic drugs strongly corresponded with their occupancy of dopamine receptors, Solomon Snyder and Philip Seeman in the 1970s may have supplied the greatest evidence to support the dopamine hypothesis.

Introduction

Schizophrenia is a mental health illness that can impair social relationships, emotional reactivity, intellectual processes, and perceptions. According to recent studies, schizophrenia is a neurodevelopmental condition with a significant dopamine component. Dopamine's role in schizophrenia has been the subject of decades of study, and it is now apparent that dopamine excesses or deficiency can cause schizophrenia symptoms.

Neurotransmitters include dopamine. Neurotransmitters are chemical messengers that let the brain, spinal cord, and peripheral nerves recognise and coordinate particular types of nerve cells. They can also deliver messages to hormone-releasing glands and muscles.When a neurotransmitter reaches a target cell, it binds to particular receptors and causes the cell to perform a specific activity. One of the most well-established theories about the pathophysiology of schizophrenia is that dopaminergic insufficiency may contribute to the emergence of psychotic symptoms.

What Does the Dopamine Schizophrenia Hypothesis Mean?

One of the earliest neurobiological hypothesis for schizophrenia was the dopamine hypothesis. According to this idea, the symptoms of schizophrenia are caused by an imbalance in the dopamine system. In other words, dopamine regulates our perception of reality, and too much or too little of it can lead to hallucinations and delusions. Animal models and post-mortem investigations showing dopamine and its metabolite abnormalities in schizophrenia patients are two sources of evidence for this notion. Additionally, medications that inhibit dopamine receptors can help manage the symptoms of schizophrenia.

Dopamine Impairment in Schizophrenia: Indirect Evidence

Rodent models of schizophrenia are helpful for examining potential pathophysiologically relevant molecular pathways. Giving repeated doses of amphetamine is one well-studied model of dopaminergic hyperactivity. These include diminished prepulse inhibition, stereotyped behaviors, and impaired cognitive flexibility and attention, all of which have been demonstrated to be induced by this. Indirect evidence for a function of dopamine in behavior assumed to be a proxy for psychotic symptoms is provided by the fact that amphetamine causes the release of dopamine and that the aforementioned effects can be mitigated by the injection of dopamine antagonists.

Other examples are related to dopaminergic disorders even if they do not directly target the dopamine system. In conclusion, a variety of techniques have been employed to boost striatal dopamine signaling in animal models, and they consistently result in behaviors that are similar to those seen in people who have schizophrenia.

How Do Dopamine-Related Schizophrenia Symptoms Occur?

There are two types of schizophrenia symptoms that an excess of dopamine may cause: positive and negative symptoms. Hallucinations and delusions are examples of positive symptoms. A decline in social engagement, emotional range, and cognitive ability are negative signs.

Positive Symptoms:

The effects of dopamine on cortical neuron's subtype-3A dopamine receptors (D3) play a role in the emergence of positive symptoms. The prefrontal cortex, which regulates thought and planning together with other parts of the brain, has the subtype-3A receptor. Dopamine-activated versions of these receptors cause neurons to become overstimulated. All three of the positive symptom kinds can result from this. Studies demonstrating that schizophrenia patients have significantly lower D3 receptor levels than healthy individuals support this theory.

Negative Symptoms:

Dopamine levels in the limbic system are related to the mechanisms that cause negative symptoms. Dopamine excess causes dopamine receptors to become more active, leading to overstimulation similar to that found in positive symptoms.

Conclusion

Dopaminergic dysregulation is not well supported by genetic studies as a main issue. Additionally, it appears that environmental influences could have a big impact on how dopaminergic dysfunction develops, there are many ways the disease can manifest. Despite its drawbacks and clear signs of malfunction, the dopamine hypothesis of schizophrenia is nevertheless widely accepted and useful.
