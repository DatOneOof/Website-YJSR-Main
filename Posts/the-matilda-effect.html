The Matilda effect

Posted by Maria Eduarda de Mattos Brandão on May 4, 2023

Have you ever heard the joke “What did Crick and Watson discover? Rosalind Franklin’s notes.”? If so, you perhaps know that Rosalind Franklin, a chemist and x-ray crystallographer, gathered the photographs and the data that helped James Watson e Francis Crick to conclude that the DNA has a double helix shape. However, in 1962, when the two scientists were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine, not a single note was written about Franklin, who had died in 1958.

Rosalind’s case is just one example of a much more complex phenomenon, called “the Matilda effect”. This effect refers to the systematic and often unconscious bias against recognizing the contributions of women in STEM fields. This term was coined by science historian Margaret W. Rossiter in 1993, who named it after Matilda Joslyn Gage, an American suffragist and abolitionist who was denied recognition for her contributions to the women's rights movement. In other words, women’s work is constantly downplayed, while their male counterparts’ projects are supported and celebrated.

As a consequence of this tendency, women in STEM and similar areas often face underfunding and lack opportunities for career advancement. According to a 2018 report of the National Science Board (NSB), women constituted half of the college-educated U.S. workforce in science and engineering, but only 28% of workers in these occupations. Moreover, 2016 data from the UNESCO Institute for Statistics (UIS) showed that less than 30% of researchers in STEM are women. Thus, the Matilda effect keeps several qualified professionals away from science for the sake of the preconceived idea that women are less efficient.

Therefore, the Matilda effect contributes to a lack of diversity and inclusivity in STEM fields. Citing the United Nations Secretary-General António Guterres, “We can all do our part to unleash our world’s enormous untapped talent – starting with filling classrooms, laboratories, and boardrooms with women scientists”. The issue of gender equality has been addressed by the United Nations in recent years, including the creation of Sustainable Development Goal 5 — “achieve gender equality and empower all women and girls” — for the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development. So, tackling this bias is a matter of urgency.

In short, the Matilda effect negatively affects the feminine professional performance by preventing female scientists and researchers from accessing laboratories and other environments related to STEM. Specialists and activist groups are now engaged in bringing this tendency to a halt, but it is not easy to reverse such a lasting and structural issue.
