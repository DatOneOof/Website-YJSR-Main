The Placebo Effect

Mind over matter: The surprising impact of perception on healing.

Posted by Charvi Kashyap on February 8, 2023

Introduction

"Placebos may make you feel better, but they will not cure you," says Professor Ted Kaptchuk. The placebo effect is a phenomenon by which the mind can deceive you into thinking that a fake treatment has actual therapeutic effects. A placebo can be a sugar pill, an injection containing saline water, or even a mock surgery. In contrast to being produced by the properties of the placebo, the placebo effect is brought on by the patient's expectation of feeling better and their belief in the therapeutic benefit. Sometimes, placebos can have an impact strong enough to resemble the results of actual medical treatments.

The conditions they have been found to be most beneficial for pain control, stress-related insomnia, and nausea and fatigue associated with cancer treatments.The study of the placebo effect, at its core, is the study of how the context of beliefs and values shape brain processes related to perception and emotion and, ultimately, mental and physical health.

Placebo in History

The use of the placebo effect in medicine has been debatable throughout history and was widespread until the middle of the 20th century. Richard Cabot came to the conclusion that it should be avoided because it is deceptive in 1903. It may be unethical to use a placebo, but it's equally wrong to "not employ something that heals," as Newman put it. He advises using the placebo effect, or the meaning response, in medicine to overcome this problem, provided the person providing it is "honest, open, and believes in its potential healing ability."

How does Placebo work exactly?

The placebo effect illustrates an important but still poorly understood link between the mind and body. We'll overlook a few psychological explanations for the placebo effect.

Expectations: A person's expectations have a significant role in the placebo effect. If you have preconceived notions about anything, they may influence how you perceive it. Therefore, if you expect getting better after taking a medication, you could. Expectations can be generated by several cues such as a doctor's positive attitude and body language can generate a sense of reassurance, making one feel positive about the treatment.

Classical Conditioning

Classical conditioning is a type of learning,It takes place when you link an object to a particular reaction. For instance, if a certain meal makes you ill, you can associate it with your illness and avoid it in future. The classical conditioning associations might influence behavior, therefore they might contribute to the placebo effect. Let's examine a few instances:

  1. A person may start to link a certain medication with pain relief if you take it to treat headaches. Due to this association, you can still experience reduced pain if you take a placebo pill that looks identical to the real thing for a headache.
  2. You might associate visiting the doctor's office to getting well or getting treatment. Your perception of the treatment you're receiving may then be affected by this association.

Genetics

Genes may also affect how individuals react to placebo therapies. Some people react more positively to placebos due to genetic predisposition. According to one study, individuals with a gene variant that codes for higher quantities of the brain chemical dopamine are more responsive to the placebo effect than individuals with the low-dopamine form. Additionally, those who carry this gene's high-dopamine variant frequently exhibit greater degrees of reward seeking and pain sensitivity.

Motor Control and the Placebo Effect: Research on Parkinson's Disease

In this instance, patients get an inert chemical (the placebo) and are informed that it is an antiparkinsonian medication that enhances motor function. In a recent study, [11C]raclopride and endogenous dopamine competed for D2/D3 receptors using positron emission tomography (PET). This research found that in the striatum of parkinsonian patients, endogenous dopamine is activated by the placebo-induced expectation of motor improvement. The therapeutic efficacy of subthalamic nucleus stimulation in parkinsonian patients who had undergone chronic electrode implantation for deep brain stimulation has also been found to be influenced by expectancies of poor versus good motor performance (DBS). In fact, when patients expected to perform well motorically, their hands moved faster, according to research on the impact of subthalamic stimulation on the speed of right-hand movement. These outcomes occurred swiftly, indicating that expectations rapidly generate brain alterations.

How the Placebo Effect Impacts Individuals

Depression

It has been discovered that persons with major depressive disorder are affected by the placebo effect. One trial gave fast-acting antidepressants or placebo pills to participants who were not currently on any other medications for a week. After a week, the subjects received an injection to improve their mood, and the researchers performed PET scans. Participants who received both the injection and the placebo pill that was marketed as an antidepressant reported less signs of depression and more activity in brain regions related to emotion and stress management.

Relieving Symptoms

The placebo effect has also been investigated in cancer survivors who experience fatigue due to their disease. Three weeks of either the participant's regular therapy or a tablet marked as a placebo were given to the participants. Despite being marketed as a placebo, the study discovered that symptoms were reported to be improved while using the medication and three weeks after stopping it.

The "Nocebo Effect", or the Reverse Placebo Effect

As mentioned above, the brain can play a significant role in the treatment or even cure of several diseases. However, the other side of this coin—a symptom or sickness brought on by anticipation or anxiety about a negative outcome—can be far more potent. The "nocebo" effect, often known as the "placebo's evil twin," has been applied to this. This reaction is used to describe negative, unpleasant, or unwanted side effects following an inactive treatment.

Conclusion

Because there is no agreed-upon definition of a placebo and it is impossible to determine the genuine justification for their usage, any discussion of what is the proper or reasonable use of placebos is made more difficult. It is generally accepted that it is unethical to intentionally mislead patients about the results of therapy. It erodes confidence in the doctor-patient relationship among other things. It is impossible to gauge the frequency with which doctors purposely mislead patients about their treatments.
