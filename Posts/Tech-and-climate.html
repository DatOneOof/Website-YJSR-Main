Technology & Climate

The Rise of CCS

Posted by Zhuri Burchenson on August 28, 2023

Introduction

Carbon Dioxide is not a foreign phrase to anyone who’s taken an elementary-level science class. It is infamous for its contributions to our earth’s greatest issues, global warming, climate change, and the greenhouse effect, all intertwined. Many have been fed the same information, carbon dioxide acts as a cap which traps heat- which should be released into space- in the atmosphere. Carbon Dioxide (CO2) is one of, if not the primary source behind climate change.

[4] One main strategy for reducing the effects of climate change is reducing carbon emissions, through acts such as turning off unused lights, carpooling and utilizing bikes and walks for reaching destinations. However, according to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), reducing emissions simply isn’t enough to make a difference. The effects of greenhouse gases active today will remain circulating throughout our atmosphere regardless of the emissions we reduce. Hence, the IPCC has confirmed that we need to find methods to remove carbon dioxide from the environment.

One such method in development is carbon capture and sequestration.

CARBON CAPTURE AND SEQUESTRATION PROCESS

The idea for the first carbon capture plant originated in 1938, and the first significant effort to inject CO2 into the ground got underway in Texas's Sharon Ridge oilfield in 1972. 24 years later, Norway began operating Sleipner in the North Sea, the first integrated carbon capture and storage project in history. There are already 43 large-scale, commercial carbon capture and storage facilities located throughout the world. 18 of these are now in use, while 16 are for industrial purposes. Carbon capture and storage (CCS) reduces carbon emissions by removing them from the atmosphere, and experts believe it may be key in the fight against global warming. First, carbon dioxide emissions from industrial processes or fossil fuels burning are transported from their point of production and then stored deep underground.

BREAKDOWN

CAPTURE

[1] Power plants, Industrial processes, gas facilities, and the open air, are sources from which CO2 is captured. In order to remove or scrub the CO2 from the environment, 3 different techniques can be applied: POST-COMBUSTION, PRE-COMBUSTION AND OXYFUEL COMBUSTION In post-combustion, as the name suggests, CO2 is removed after the fossil fuel is burnt. It takes place in most, if not all power plants and the CO2 is scrubbed from the exhaust. This technology is the most reliable and understood,d and also applied in other scenarios. In pre-combustion, CO2 is trapped before the fuel is burnt. The fossil fuel is partially burnt, forming synthetic gas, which CO2 can now be easily captured from. This technique cannot be applied to power plants, but, however, works perfectly for fertilizer, gas and power production. In oxyfuel combustion, oxygen is used to burn fossil fuels rather than air. Cooling causes the water to condense, producing nearly pure CO2 that may be transported and stored. Oxyfuel combustion technologies used in electricity plants are commonly referred to as "zero emission" procedures since almost all of the CO2 is collected. Since it's likely that some CO2 will dissolve in the condensed water, additional treatment steps may be necessary. Even though it might be the most efficient approach of the three, the initial oxygen-burning process requires a lot of energy.

TRANSPORT

[1] CO2 is then transferred to suitable storage sites. Pipelines are one of the cheapest and most commonly used transport systems. Pipelines in the United States facilitate oil production, where the extracted CO2 is injected, producing more oil. For small-scale transport and application, ships and road tankers are famously acquired.

STORAGE

[1] There are various ways in which the extracted CO2 can be stored, however, deep geological storage and mineral storage are the main ways. Deep geological formations are also referred to as geo-sequestration. Through geo-sequestration, CO2 is converted to a high, pressure, liquid-resembling form, which is referred to as supercritical CO2. This semi-runny liquid is injected into sedimentary rocks. The CO2 is considered trapped, and unlikely to seep out. Unmineable coal, that is, coal which is unable or too difficult to be mined, is also a storage location for extracted CO2. Permeable coal allows CO2 to penetrate, which causes the coal to then release methane, which is recovered and used for fuels in various systems. Saline aquifers are another storage location, serving as deep rock formations with highly concentrated salt water which acts as a sponge to absorb CO2. These are one of the most advantageous storage methods, as saline aquifers are large in abundance and have large storage potential. However, as they produce no byproduct which reaps profit, they are said to be more expensive.


RISKS OF CARBON CAPTURE

POTENTIAL LEAKS

Based on the above-described methods, one’s mind would immediately jump to the possibility of leakage. The truth is, if CO2 fails to remain in a liquid state, it is more than likely to leek and seep into surroundings areas. These leaks pose environmental and safety issues, ie soil contamination and pollution. This leaking gas may also seep into populated areas, posing health risks to those in the vicinity.

DANGEROUS INFRASTRUCTURE BUILD

Infrastructure involved with CCS also poses an issue. Pipelines, mentioned above, are notorious for disrupting local ecosystems and displacing wildlife. Additionally, pipelines are vulnerable to several natural disasters, meaning that if impacted by such, CO2 is likely to leak and evoke hazardous results.

PUBLIC PERCEPTION

The way the general public views CCS is also significant. How will people react if a ground-level injection of an invisible gas occurs close to their homes? Would they have faith that it would be kept secure and free from any threat of leakage or escape? How will businesses explain CCS to a population of consumers that are growing more concerned?


EVALUATION OF EFFICIENCY

While generally perceived as a breakthrough in the fight against climate change, different experts hold differing views on the long-term impacts of CCS. Mark Z. Jacobson(Staford University), has suggested that CCS can cause more harm, than good. [6] To compare the actual and potential efficiencies of these two types of plants, Jacobson looked at a number of scenarios, including what would happen if the carbon capture technologies were powered by renewable electricity rather than natural gas and if the same amount of renewable electricity needed to power the equipment were instead used to replace the coal plant's electricity.

Although the effectiveness of carbon capture technology is typically estimated to be between 85 and 90 per cent, neither of these facilities achieved that goal. Even without taking into consideration upstream emissions, the average efficiency of the coal plant's equipment over a 6-month period was only 55.4%. [5] The cost of the equipment and also the processes involved, are also greatly expensive, outweighing many of the pros associated with the procedure.[3] The Global CCS Institute’s report asserts that the biggest obstruction to realizing the full potential of carbon capture and storage technology is that the market still does not provide any substantial incentives for companies to gain a full return on investment. The need of the hour is policies that fully support investments in carbon capture and storage.[2]
