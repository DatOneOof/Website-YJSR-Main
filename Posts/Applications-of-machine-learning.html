Applications Of Machine Learning in Healthcare

Posted by Jingran Yang on November 3, 2022 Edited by Sangeetha Sree Selva Kumar

Introduction

  • As we live in the age of big data, the applications of machine learning, a subset of artificial intelligence, are becoming increasingly more prominent in our society. The technology is now used in Netflix’s recommendation system, our phone’s voice recognition functionality, and the bank’s software to detect suspicious transactions, etc. Machine learning also plays an important role in health care, ranging from handling the medical records of patients to assisting physicians in making critical decisions.
  • Various modern companies have applied machine learning to improve the public’s healthcare experience. For example, Microsoft’s Project InnerEye focuses on using computer vision to help radiologists to detect tumors using 3D radiological images. Companies like Kensci use machine learning to predict illness, allowing physicians to intervene early and prevent patients from contracting health issues. Additionally, the Subtle Medical company employs deep learning to reduce image noises on medical images to speed up the process of diagnosis and treatment for patients.

Machine Learning for Covid Diagnosis

  • One recent development of machine learning in healthcare is the automated detection of Covid-19 through convolutional neural networks (CNN) using X-ray images of the chest. The study aims to develop a reliable detection of Covid-19 through deep learning-based systems and evaluate the performance of its classification technique.

    • Figure 1

  • The researchers within this study chose the CNN model because it was proven powerful at classifying patients according to their risks of developing a disease. The CNN model is one of the most studied machine learning algorithms for medical diagnosis using images.
  • To develop and train CNN capable of diagnosing Covid-19 from scratch, researchers used a public dataset that includes X-Ray chest images for both training and validating the model.

    • Figure 2

  • The gathered dataset includes 1341 images for individuals without Covid-19, 3875 images for individuals with bacterial Pneumonia, and 296 images for individuals with or suspected of Covid. It is important to note that these images are not distributed evenly across the three categories, requiring the researchers to also deal with the problem of imbalance to avoid misleading classification.

    • Figure 3

  • After processing the dataset to improve the model’s classification accuracy, the researchers trained, fine-tuned, and evaluated the model’s performance at correctly detecting signs of Covid-19. The CNN architecture which was trained and built from scratch yielded an accuracy of 97.62% and 93.75% for binary and muti-class classification respectively.

Machine Learning for Brain Tumor Diagnosis

  • Another noteworthy application of machine learning in healthcare is the automatic detection of brain tumors from Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI) scans.
  • Automatic classification of brain tumors has proved challenging due to the variation in sizes and shapes of brain tumors. The difference in appearance between brain tumors made it difficult for machines to classify them correctly.

    • Figure 4

  • For this study, researchers used a CNN-based classifier similar to the previous study in order to perform the automatic detection of brain tumors. The CNN model is advantageous because it provides a fully automatic classification system that doesn’t require manual segmentation of tumor regions. Previous research also proved that the model works well with MRI scans.
  • To train and validate the CNN classifier, researchers used an open-source dataset with a total of 3064 brain MRI images. Within the 3064 brain MRI images, there were 1426 images with glioma tumors, 708 images with meningioma tumors, and 930 images with pituitary tumors.
  • After training and fine-tuning the CNN classifier, the researchers achieved an average accuracy of 98%, which outperformed previous studies using different methods. The resulting CNN classifier was fully automatic, provided exceptional accuracy, and required little manual pre-processing to be done on the MRI brain images.

Conclusion

The applications of machine learning in healthcare provide exceptional potential. Machines are proven capable of diagnosing diseases such as Covid-19 and detecting brain tumors with remarkable accuracy. By utilizing this technology, physicians will be able to intervene early before the patient’s medical condition worsens, avoiding potential misdiagnosis due to human errors.
