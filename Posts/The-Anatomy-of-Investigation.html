The Anatomy of Investigation

Bridging The Gap Between Science and Justice

Posted by Lorel Gonzales on November 22, 2023

Over time, our understanding of how to investigate crime has widely advanced over several centuries. In modern times, it takes a whole team of individuals to precisely piece together a labyrinth of clues to bring justice to a prevailing crime.

One of the most important parts of a criminal investigation is body evidence. Even the smallest piece of evidence deriving from victims or criminals can unravel a whole yarn ball of attestation in a criminal case. And turns out, there's a significant string between knowing the anatomy of the human body and resolving the intricacies of a criminal case.

Many components of the human body are significant, the blood in our veins and arteries, the unique indentations on the tips of our fingers, or traces of the hair on our head. Our body plays an immense role in a crime, more than we know it. Because of this, crime lab scientists have developed numerous methods specifically for evidence that derives from human anatomy.

The hard structured frame of our body, the skeletal system. The skeletal frame of someone serves as a frame for forensic scientists. Our skeletal system is unique to us, and is shaped by many things including, genetics, lifestyle, and environment. A skeletal frame can help establish the profile of the victim. Another form of analyzing the bones and skeletal formation is the trauma that it was subjected to. The indentation, or indentations, of the skeletal structure could be a substantial giveaway to what exact weapon was used to harm the victim.

Another common component when discovering evidence with anatomy is the crimson streams that flow all throughout our body. Blood can be widely used as physical evidence of a crime taking place in a certain spot; in the forms of splatters, pools, stains on clothing, etc. Moreover, with the advanced technology that we have developed over the years, we can now match certain blood types to the one found at the crime scene. These types of DNA profilings have revolutionized criminal investigation, either to narrow down suspects or to prove a suspect guilty with the certitude of science.

Lastly, the time of death concerning a deceased victim is a key aspect to framing exactly when the crime occurred. Luckily, the human body has several biochemical, anatomical, and physical indicators of when exactly the crime has taken place. The three mortises, for example; rigor mortis, livor mortis, and algor mortis are all post-mortem changes identifiable by a forensic pathologist. They are all innate causes that happen entirely because of the nature of the human body itself.

Rigor mortis is the stiffening of muscles caused by a drop in adenosine triphosphate (ATP) which plays a role in muscle relaxation. It usually starts small, and as time passes spreads throughout the entire body. By analyzing the degree of rigor mortis, it can be a sign of the onset of the depletion of ATP. Livor mortis is when the blood sets due to gravitational forces in the body. In livor mortis, blood tends to pool in usually resistant areas, causing the skin to have a purple hue. Like rigor mortis, the level of hue of someone's skin in certain areas can be an index into how long someone has been lamented. And finally, algor mortis is when the body undergoes a change in temperature. After an individual dies, the body no longer has ways of cooling itself or producing bodily heat, so it regulates to about room temperature.

Once again, our body is important for a multitude of things in everyday life, both for ourselves and others. In the case of crime, it is important to use every last bit of evidence to bring justice to a victim, and their loved ones. And you will find, the human bodies and all its complexity is useful for bridging a gap between science, and finding justice.
