Theories of Relativity

Is time travel possible?

Posted by Ishika Sinha on September 18, 2023 Edited by Mukund Gupta

Time travel is a notion which has been utilised in science fiction and movies for decades. An example of this is the movie Interstellar by Christopher Nolan which depicts the influence of an intense gravitational field on Matthew McConaughey’s crew, slowing time down for them. The discussions of time travel were initiated within physics following Einstein’s special theory of relativity.

Einstein’s theories of relativity

Einstein developed two theories of relativity : General theory of relativity and Special theory of relativity.

General relativity

Gravity may initially be seen as a force which acts by attracting objects according to their mass. The larger the mass, the more other objects will be pulled towards it. For example, planets orbiting the sun. However, certain examples show that gravity is not a force. As an example, rather than falling straight down towards the centre of the earth as we would expect, satellites’ trajectories are curved, following the direction of rotation of the earth. If we were to continue with the theory that gravity is not a force, our current understanding of the universe would have to undergo a radical change. This formed the basis of general relativity.

General relativity is based on the equivalence principle which states that at any point in space-time, gravitational and inertial effects cannot be distinguished. An example of this is freefall as freefall is not the acceleration of the object but rather the object’s natural movement. It is freefall which is our natural motion rather than rest. When we are standing on the Earth’s surface, we are resistant to freefall, exerting a constant upward acceleration. This explains why we feel our weight only when we touch the ground. [1] All objects which have a mass curve spacetime around them. If we were to send an object such as an apple into intergalactic emptiness, it would travel in a straight line at constant velocity. However, if we were to place Earth in this space and observe the motions of the apple now, it would present a curved trajectory towards Earth as due to Earth’s mass, it has distorted spacetime, leading the apple to arrive towards it.

Special relativity

This theory concerns the actions of objects as they approach the speed of light, delving into concepts such as time dilation and length contraction.

Time dilation

The faster you move, the slower time ticks for you, relative to the observer. Not only does this occur for motion, but it occurs depending on the gravitational field strength you experience as well. The stronger the gravitational field, the slower time ticks for you. Focusing on the motion aspect, if one were to speed up, approaching the speed of light, time would tick slower and slower. This is called time dilation and it is not a merely hypothetical concept but rather one that has been tested and proved to be real by the 1971 Hafele-Keating experiments. In these experiments, two precisely measured atomic clocks were flown on planes travelling in opposite directions. The relative motion had a measurable impact, creating a time difference between the two clocks.[4]

The stationary time is the time measured in a stationary frame of reference As well as the experiments with atomic clocks on planes, this phenomenon has also been observed with particle decay. For example, a muon decays with a lifetime of 2.2*10-6 as measured in the lab. If a muon is moving at 99% of the speed of light, its lifetime, using the formula above would be 1.56*10-5. Furthermore, if a particle were to have a decay time of 9 minutes and it is greatly accelerated in a particle accelerator, its decay time would increase. It would decay after a certain amount of time which is the exact amount of time that was predicted by Einstein. [3] Rather than going at 99% of the speed of light, what if one were to travel at the speed of light? Photons already do this. As they already travel at this high speed, photons do not experience time. A photon being emitted from a star 30,000 years ago and then landing on a telescope will have happened in exactly the same instant relative to the photon.[3]

Length contraction

At very high speeds, within 10% of the speed of light, the length of a moving object seen by a stationary observer shortens. The faster you travel, the smaller objects will seem to be as well as the distance you perceive yourself to be travelling. This would mean that it is possible to travel thousands of light years within just a few seconds. For example, we can imagine a rocket ship travelling from Earth to a distant celestial object. The two perspectives we can view are from an observer on Earth and from a person travelling in a rocket ship. When the Earth is the inertial reference frame, the ship is moving at constant velocity and time is moving at the familiar rate. The distance for the journey is able to be measured as the ‘proper length’. This is from an observer who is at rest with respect to the start and endpoints. [5] Using the ship as the inertial reference frame, the only thing that is the same is the relative velocity of the ship to the Earth and to the celestial object. The observer in the ship is in motion with respect to the start and endpoints. A practical example of Einstein’s special relativity would be GPS satellites. These satellites orbit far away enough from Earth to have a measurable difference in their spacetime conditions. The Earth’s gravitational field is weaker for these satellites than for us on Earth, meaning that time ticks more slowly for them. They gain around 38 microseconds per day due to time dilation. Despite this, we’re still able to receive accurate measurements from them because the time differences are corrected to adjust to our spacetime continuum.

Is time travel possible?

The effects of time dilation could potentially allow us to see into the future. We observe the stars from Earth as they were billions of years ago, meaning that we essentially look into the past when looking at the sky. However, for us to utilise this, we would have to take advantage of the strong gravitational field of black holes or send a traveller in a rocket ship at close to the speed of light. Our current technology has not permitted us to do this as of yet but in the future with more advanced technology, this could be possible. [2]
