Where can we find 2D/3D animation in our daily lives?

Posted by Anel Mukatova on September 7, 2023 Edited by Alissa Ye

Animation, an art form continually evolving for centuries, involves creating motion and/or interaction of elements in a two-dimensional or three-dimensional environment. Every animation is unique and involves different methods of creating them, such as briefing, scripting, voice-over, audio mix, and sign-off (Torre, 2017). Today, animation finds applications in entertainment’s, education, advertising, gaming, and other domains (Ho, 2019).

Naturally, animation plays a major role in marketing. It is an instrument that combines technical and marketing tools to capture the audience's attention. For example, Android released an advertisement introducing an anti-bullying storyline that fascinated the public with its unique 3D animation of a classic game – rock, paper, scissors (Song, 2017). This demonstrates animations’ usage of convincing information through storytelling, aesthetics, and movements. Therefore, companies often use animations with breath-taking scenarios to create eye-catching visuals that stand out from traditional 2D advertisements (Schiele et al., 2020). However, the ubiquitous use of animation undervalues its importance and reduces its efficacy. Because people are surrounded by advertisements everywhere, they resist focusing on them, acting as a less permeable filter in preference formation. These realizations are inevitable but allow the animation to progress and technologically advance, bringing ideas to people (Cook and Thompson, 2019). Ultimately, animation in advertising maintains some equilibrium of prominence in daily life, where people notice the impressive examples but rarely any others.

Animation and AI have been increasingly interconnected in recent years. As deep learning continues its rapid development and becomes more widely adopted within the industry, it brings significant changes in how animations are created. (Hung et al., 2020). Animations generated through machine learning could eventually become indistinguishable from those crafted by human hands – something that would revolutionize animated filmmaking and gaming experiences. Deep learning algorithms analyze characters' faces from various angles, detect expressions and movements, and create accurate representations as the foundation for animation (Hung et al., 2020). For example, Google's “DeepDream” has been used by animators to produce abstract visuals inspired by neural networks (Cox, 2019). Animation and reality is becoming blurred through these constant enhancements, and in daily life, it is becoming increasingly less noticeable.

Animation is also used in industries like healthcare, particularly 3D animation. One common place where we encounter 3D animation is in medical imaging techniques, including CT scans and MRIs. By combining hardware and software, doctors can create detailed images, offering an inside look at the human body without invasive procedures (Udupa, 1999). Dentists also employ these techniques to provide accurate X-ray images for diagnosing dental problems (Dawood, 2015). These technologies are extensively used by professionals for training purposes, like teaching medical students about anatomy or surgical techniques (Udupa, 1999).

In conclusion, animation has become an integral part of our lives, both professionally and personally. From creating captivating visuals in social media and advertisements to assisting doctors during surgeries or teachers within classrooms,we find ourselves surrounded by this innovative form of art every day – whether we realize it or not.
