Surgical Robots

A New Age for Minimally Invasive Surgeries

Posted by Lisara Gunawardana on August 5, 2023

Robot-assisted surgery is a fairly new concept, with the first ever record of it being performed during a brain biopsy in the 1980s, by a robot known as PUMA 560 (Moore, 2018). Since then, technology has advanced at an exponential rate, allowing robots to be fitted with newer, more specialised features such as endoscopic cameras and infrared sensors.

Why Robotic Surgery?

The reason as to why some may prefer robotic surgery, over traditional surgery, may be due to the fact that robots can carry out minimally invasive procedures and move with greater precision. This presents itself as a benefit to patients since smaller incisions help minimise the risk of complications post-surgery (such as infection and blood loss), as well as reducing pain during recovery. Moreover, a study conducted by the University of Sheffield and UCL (University College London) concluded that robot-assisted surgery enabled patients, who were undergoing bladder cancer removal, to recover at a faster rate and therefore spend 20% less time in hospital as a result (UCL, 2022). This is a significant decrease which not only poses as a positive to patients, but also to hospitals struggling with high occupancy rates and bed shortages, highlighting the benefits of introducing surgical systems to healthcare facilities.

On the other hand, there are a few drawbacks to surgical robots, the primary one being the costs of purchasing and maintaining expensive robotic technology. In addition to this, surgeons need to be specially trained in order to operate the robots; this usually includes undergoing a comprehensive training course, which involves simulations, theory modules and supervised operations (Hazelden, 2021). If a surgeon does not gain adequate experience operating the device, the effectiveness and safety of the operation could be put at risk. Furthermore, an article detailing the drawbacks of da Vinci Robotic Surgery (DrugDangers, 2022) states that “the use of robot-assisted technology may not come naturally to all physicians”, which could be due to the fact that the use of robots in the medical field is quite new. As a consequence, surgeon inexperience has been identified as a leading cause of error during robotic surgery.

Although these malfunctions can be detrimental, a law firm specialising in medical malpractice claims that “errors only occur in about 0.1-0.5% of all robotic surgeries”, which is a “substantially lower rate” compared to that of mistakes made by surgeons during traditional surgery (Caputo, 2020). Therefore, surgical robots are usually seen as an efficacious and safer alternative to traditional surgery in most cases.

The Future of Robotic Surgery

Since the introduction of surgical robots in the 1980s, their effectiveness only seeks to increase with the development of AI. Currently, robots can be equipped with computer vision, providing a high-quality 3D image that is much more effective than the naked eye, giving the surgeon an almost ‘superhuman strength’. In the future, it is likely that there will be an emphasis on “expanding the scope of robotics,” so that it is prevalent in a wider range of medical specialities (Mayor and Coppola and Challacombe, 2022). In addition to this, efforts are being made towards achieving fully automated robots; in 2022, the fully autonomous robot STAR was able to complete “one of the most intricate and delicate tasks in abdominal surgery”, by suturing two ends of pig intestine at Johns Hopkins University (Koetsier, 2022). This breakthrough helps support the idea that in the near future, surgical robots may be able to carry out low-risk procedures entirely on their own. For that reason, with the constant evolution of medical technology, it will be interesting to see what a profession in the healthcare sector may look like a few decades from now.
