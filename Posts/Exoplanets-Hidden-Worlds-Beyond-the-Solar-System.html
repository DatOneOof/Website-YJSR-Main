Exoplanets

Hidden Worlds Beyond the Solar System

Posted by Shreya Bose on August 25, 2023

Introduction

For hundreds of years, humans speculated that planets exist outside of our solar system. This was only confirmed thirty years ago when two planets, Poltergeist and Phobetor, were found orbiting a pulsar. Just two years later, a third planet by the name of Draugr was found in the same system. Named exoplanets, these planets were defined as planets outside of the solar system which usually orbited another star. This opened many news doors and theories in the world of astronomy. Since the first discovery in 1992, NASA has confirmed more than 5,000 exoplanets in the Milky Way. With the help of instruments—such as the Kepler Space Telescope and the James Webb Telescope—astronomers have researched the potential of life beyond the Earth. Calculating size, weight, and distance, scientists created a system to classify exoplanets into four main categories: Super-Earths, Gas Giants, Neptunian Planets, and Terrestrial Planets. Exoplanets are categorized by their similar elements, but Although they were grouped because of their similar elements, they still vary drastically from one another. This has led to the ultimate question of whether these planets will one day be able to support human life.

Gas Giants

As taken from the name, Gas Giants are composed of gasses such as helium and hydrogen. They usually do not have any solid surfaces, except for a solid core at the very center. These exoplanets are most similar to Jupiter and Saturn, however they are much larger. Sometimes, these planets are referred to as Hot Jupiters because they orbit very close to their stars causing temperatures to go up to thousands of degrees. Because they are so close to their stars, Gas Giants have a small orbit. Some can finish a full orbit in just a couple hours! However, this can cause the Gas Giant to be unstable and create a “wobble” in space. This means that the planet pulls on other stellar objects nearby and can cause a shift in the spectrum of light observed. This is helpful when trying to discover an exoplanet.

Figure 1: A red gas giant

Gas Giants were the first type of exoplanets discovered, and still continue the growth of astronomical discoveries. Formations of planets for hundreds of years relied on the planets in our Solar System. However, the discovery of Gas Giants have allowed scientists to hypothesize other ways planets can form. This is because our most similar planets, Jupiter and Saturn, have faraway orbits from the Sun. It was originally hypothesized that the planets were formed farther away. However, with the discovery of Gas Giants, it may be possible that Jupiter and Saturn were formed much closer to the Sun and drifted further away over the course of billions of years.

Neptunian Planets

Inferred by the label, Neptunian Planets are gaseous and are similar in size to Neptune and Uranus. Neptune, in comparison to Earth, has about four times its radius. However, some Neptunian planets have been found to be smaller than Neptune with similar composition. Discoveries such as these help open the door to new theories of planet formation as they have never been observed before. Almost 2,000 Neptunian planets have been confirmed thus far, making it one of the most common exoplanets in the Milky Way. Neptune and Uranus are often referred to as “Ice Giants.” The composition elements, such as water, methane, and ammonia are frozen in the outer layers of these planets. In 2014, the first “Ice Giant” in our Milky Way was discovered. Along with this, Neptunian Planets that are heated up to 2,000 degrees were also discovered. This leads to theorization of the formation of Neptunian Planets. It may be possible that Neptune and Uranus have been transformed into some of the coldest planets in the Solar System, but were once warmer.

Terrestrial Planets

Planets such as Mars, Venus, and Mercury would be considered terrestrial, or rocky, planets. Outside of the Solar System, terrestrial planets are between ½ to twice the size of the Earth. They are composed of rocks, silicate, and carbon. Terrestrial planets are most similar to Super-Earths, the only difference being their sizes. Their composition is mainly rock, silicate, water, and carbon. A bulk of their composition is iron, and they have been found with liquid on their surfaces as well. It is estimated that over 10 billion Terrestrial Planets exist in just the Milky Way alone! In 2017, one solar system by the name Trappist-1, had seven Terrestrial Planets. These planets look the most promising when searching for habitability. Most of them have been found within the habitable zone of their star. This means that they are within the distance range of the star to exhibit a temperature similar to that of Earth. Many of them may also have more water than Earth because of the amount of water vapor in their atmospheres. Scientists hope that with more advanced telescopes, these planet’s can be studied more closely.

Hunt for Exoplanets

How do we find exoplanets? Although scientists have only searched a small area of the cosmic scale, these exoplanets are still thousands of light years away from Earth. How is it possible to detect planets that far? Well, researchers have five main ways to spot them. As mentioned previously, Gas Giants can be so large they cause a wobble in space. This can be detected from Earth, and is known as radial velocity. This is one of the most common ways to find exoplanets. The most common, known as transiting, is when a planet passes between its star and the Earth. This causes a dimming of the star’s light, allowing scientists to confirm a planet’s presence. Over 4,000 exoplanets have been discovered this way. Direct imaging is when astronomers take pictures of the planets from Earth. They use powerful tools to be able to capture the exoplanet in space and avoid the star’s glare. Another method, known as gravitational lensing, is one of the most interesting. When gravity is present, it can cause bends in how light travels. Detecting these bends in lights can confirm that an object of mass, an exoplanet, is there. Lastly, one of the least efficient methods of exoplanet detection is astrometry. Only two exoplanets have been discovered by this method. It is when a star starts to wobble in space in relation to other stars nearby. This allows scientists to detect the movement of exoplanets orbiting it.

Conclusion

Although exoplanet research has been ongoing for decades, there are still many unknowns about them. These worlds have the chance of containing new chemical elements, different explanations for planet formation, and possibly life. They have and still continue to raise new theories for interstellar objects and their origins. Humanity can only hope to continue their advancements in astronomical discoveries and study the worlds that reside outside of our solar system.
