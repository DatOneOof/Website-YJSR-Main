Photocells

Posted by Afreen Hossain on August 1, 2023

Lights, electrifying photoelectric marvels, let's dive into the fascinating world of photocells. A concept that can captivate beginners. You might have heard about Heinrich Rudolf Hertz's incredible discovery known as the photoelectric effect. For those who aren't familiar, let me give you a simple explanation.

When a specific type of radiation, usually light, shines onto the surface of a metal like zinc, it causes electrons on the metal's surface to be emitted. These electrons, called free electrons, exist on conductive surfaces and can only be released when they absorb a certain amount of energy.

To release these electrons, the frequency of the light must exceed a minimum threshold frequency required for electron emission. At this point, there is a positively charged collector present that attracts and collects the emitted electrons from the metal. This process creates a potential difference, generating an electric field. One practical application of the photoelectric effect is the use of photocells. Despite their small size, photocells are primarily used to detect the presence of electric current. Due to their compact nature, they can detect very small units of current, often measured in microamperes (10^-6 amperes).

To understand the practical application of photocells, let's consider an example. When we visit malls or other places, we often encounter sliding doors that automatically open as we approach them. In this scenario, a specific radiation with an intensity denoted as "i" continuously illuminates the photocell. The light is invisible to us. As a result, current is generated within the photocell. There may also be a sensor that detects the presence of this current, keeping the sliding doors closed while the current flows. However, when a person approaches the doors, they unintentionally block the light that was previously falling on the photocell. Rest assured, the light used is harmless. As a result, the current in the photocell stops flowing, and the sensor quickly detects this change. The sensor then communicates with another sensor, signalling the doors to open.

Let's explore another example that highlights the significant advantages of using photocells, particularly in burglar alarms. When we visit museums or places where highly valuable items are displayed, numerous photocells are often installed around them. These photocells are continuously exposed to light in a way that prevents any sound from being produced. However, if someone tries to reach their hand toward the valuable item, as if attempting to steal it, the light falling on the photocells gets blocked. This interruption in light flow causes the current in the photocells to be disrupted, triggering a sensor to detect this change. Consequently, loud ringing and alarm sounds are activated by the burglar alarms.

Isn't it incredible how fascinating photoelectric cells are? It's a reminder of how amazing physics can be. We'll definitely be back with more lessons in the future. Stay tuned.
