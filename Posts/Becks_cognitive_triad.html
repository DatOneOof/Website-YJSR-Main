Beck’s Cognitive Triad: Self-Schemas, Cognitive Bias and Negative Triad

A cognitive-therapeutic view of depression.

Posted by Jiji Subhan on October 8, 2023 Edited by Kritika Parashar

Introduction

Mood disorders are a vital aspect of clinical psychology, and we must understand the possible explanations for them in the modern world so that we can help ourselves as well as those around us. An estimated 3.8% of the population suffer from depression, including 5% of adults (4% among men and 6% among women), and 5.7% of adults older than 60 years. According to the WHO, 280 million people worldwide suffer from depression.

Beck’s Cognitive triad

Beck developed a cognitive explanation for depression that helps us understand the underlying psychological mechanisms behind depression. Beck’s explanation includes 3 components:

  1. cognitive bias
  2. negative self-schemas
  3. negative triad

Beck suggested that people suffering from depression are prone to distorting and misinterpreting information, a process known as cognitive bias. They will mostly misinterpret information negatively and ignore the positive aspects of something. The two most crucial examples of cognitive biases are over-generalization and catastrophizing.

Over-generalization is when individuals draw negative conclusions from a single setback or event in their lives. For example when someone didn’t do well in one of their exams and they conclude from that event that they will not do well in all exams.

Catastrophizing, which doctors also call “magnifying”, involves a person fixating on the worst possible outcome and treating it as likely, even when it is not. For example, a person who did not get a job assumes that he will never get a job and stops going for interviews.

Schemas, in simple words, can be defined as a mental framework by which one views the world. Schemas are developed in childhood and store the information about how we perceive the world around us. We use our schemas to learn and think more quickly. However, some schemas may also be stereotypes that cause us to misinterpret or incorrectly recall information. Beck suggested that negative beliefs about the self, the world, and the future incorporated in stable cognitive schemas are the key vulnerability factors to depression. Negative schemas are developed due to negative past experiences such as criticism by teachers or parents or being made fun of by classmates.

Cognitive processes involved in a depressive disorder can be understood to form a triad. Beck claimed that the negative schemas leading to cognitive bias, maintain a triad known as “Beck’s cognitive triad”.

In which the first component involves an individual’s view of themselves, for example an individual thinking that they are worthless.
And the second component involves negative views about the world, for example, “everyone hates me because I am worthless”, misinterpreting external forces as being against them.
Lastly, the third component is a negative view of the future for example “I will never be good at anything because everyone hates me”.

Aaron T. Beck developed CBT as a treatment in the 1970s. Cognitive behavioral therapy can be used as a treatment for a variety of mental health disorders. It is a form of talking therapy.

CBT helps people understand the psychological mechanisms behind Mental health disorders and identify when they make negative interpretations leading to negative consequences in terms of behaviors and emotions. Beck introduced cognitive restructuring which aims to gain entry into the person’s cognitive organization.

Cognitive restructuring, in simple words, is a talking therapy that involves identifying logical thinking and then changing the way a person thinks. This therapy is performed in a one-to-one interaction between an individual having depressive disorder and the therapist.

In simple words, the process can be described in 5 stages:

  1. The therapist explains the theory of cognitive triad to the patient.
  2. Training the patient to observe and record their irrational thoughts.
  3. The patient understanding the link between their thoughts, affect, and behavior.
  4. The patient will “catch” their negative thoughts or identify such ideas immediately.
  5. The therapist will use techniques of reattribution.
